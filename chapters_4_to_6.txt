Chapter 4
Proposed Approach
In this chapter, we will present our approach to the problem of information retrieval in
low-resource languages. We will first present the pipeline for training and describe the
components in the pipeline to provide a general view of how the components work and
interact with each other. From there, we will present the architecture of the model we
propose.
4.1
Problem Statement
Developing an information retrieval system for low-resource languages presents several
technical hurdles, particularly when constructing a semantically accurate model with lim-
ited training data. While one might consider translation-based approaches where queries
or documents are translated into a high-resource language such as English, this strategy
introduces several issues: poor translation quality for LRLs, semantic drift when translat-
ing from culturally rich contexts, and importantly, translating documents to high-resource
languages creates a tendency to marginalize the original language, discouraging documents
from continue to be written in that language. This undermines long-term language and
culture preservation aim of the project.
To address these issues, this project approaches in an alternative direction: designing
an IR model that operates directly within the low-resource language, without relying on
translation. By employing techniques such as knowledge distillation and query expansion,
the system transfers understanding from a high-resource model and learns to perform
retrieval in low-resource settings without relying on large manually labeled corpora. These
design choices allow the model to better capture the linguistic and cultural nuances of
the target languages, making retrieval more relevant and empowering continued digital
engagement in underrepresented language communities.

4.2
Training Pipeline
Figure 4.1: Pipeline for training process
Figure 4.1 provides an overview of the components in the pipeline and their execution
flow. As can be seen in the figure, the pipeline consists of two major parts: the knowledge
distillation part, and the training part for mono-lingual information retrieval.
As mentioned in [25], the aim of knowledge distillation is to extend existing sentence
embedding models to new languages, which help us to create multilingual versions from
previously monolingual models. In [25], they use mean-squared loss function during train-
ing. Here, we decided to experiment with a different approach for training, which is to
use the optimal transport distillation. This is the approach presented in [40]. We will
describe this approach in detail in the Subsection 4.2.1.
After the knowledge distillation part, we should obtain a language model that is ex-
pected to be good enough to encode the information of a sentence in the language of the
student model. We will then use this trained model for the mono-lingual information re-
trieval training process. In the mono-lingual information retrieval training part, we build
our pipeline based on the idea of using both lexical matching and semantic searching
which was mention in [24], combined with the use of query expansion component and
some adjustments related to the similarity scoring process between query and documents.
The execution flow goes through the following steps:
1. First, the queries in the query-document dataset along with the documents in the
64

--- Page 66 ---
document corpus, which are usually stored in raw text, go through preprocessing.
This process includes steps such as striping extra spaces, stemming, stop-word re-
moving, words and sentences segmentating, and tokenizing. The result of this step
will be documents and queries that have been processed and saved in a form more
suitable for training. For documents that are marked as relevant documents for each
query in the query-document dataset, they will be used to determine positive samples
for training.
2. After preprocessing, for each sample in the query-document set, the query is passed to
the query expansion component. The goal of this component is to create an extended
query with more terms, thereby increasing the ability to match more documents
when performing lexical matching. A detailed description for this query expansion
component will be presented later in the Subsection 4.2.2.
3. With the newly obtained expanded query, both the original query and the expanded
query are then passed independently into the lexical matching component.
This
component will calculate the lexical similarity between the query and each document
in the document corpus. The result of this step will be a list with each element being
the lexical similarity score between the query and each document in the document
corpus, sorted in descending order for convenience of subsequent components. We
will talk more about this component in the Subsection 4.2.3.
4. The following step after lexical matching is the document chunking step. In the
approach mentioned in [24], they just concatenate the title and content of each
article in the legal corpus into one ”very long” sentence. While they do mention the
percentage of articles based on length, they do not mention how to handle articles
with word counts exceeding the model’s limit, nor do they mention splitting the
articles. Meanwhile, we argue that for documents of different lengths, longer lengths
may either provide more details relevant to the query, or may contain more irrelevant
content and reduce the relevance and usefulness of the document. Within the same
document, some parts may be more relevant to the query than others, and the parts
that are truly relevant to the query may be distributed in later positions of the
document. Therefore, we believe that seperating the document into small chunks,
calculating the query relevance to each chunk, and then combining them may help
solve the above problems.
5. Along with the sample preparation process, similar to [24], we can perform the
fine-tuning process of the language model before using this model for the semantic
similarity process. Normally, with languages with few resources such as Khmer, we
will rarely have a language model optimized specifically for that language but often
65

--- Page 67 ---
have to use multilingual models (such as mBERT, XLM-R, ...). Therefore, fine-tuning
these multilingual models to make them more compatible with the language we want
to use is necessary. In addition, similar to [24], if we target the model to documents
belonging to a few specific topics (like in [24] is legal documents), we can also fine-
tuning to make the model more compatible. However, in our current approach this
step is optional, since we have already used a student model that has been distilled
from a teacher model which is already effective in representing semantics, and also
sometimes the language used already has a good enough native language model, or
the document topic is so broad that fine-tuning may lead to overfitting. In [24], they
use the legal corpus to fine-tune masked language modeling (MLM) the language
model. Referring to our case, it is similar that we will use the document corpus to
MLM fine-tuning the language model. Then we use the fine-tuned model as the base
model for the semantic searching step. If we don’t do fine-tuning, we can directly
use the language model as the base model.
6. Finally, for each query and list of documents selected as samples (each document has
been divided into chunks), they will be passed into the semantic searching training
loop.
The main component of this step is the Sentence Transformer model [21],
which will help us calculate the relevance between the query and each chunk in each
document. Description of how this and other components work will be presented in
the Subsection 4.2.4.
4.2.1
Knowledge Distillation
In this first component, we aim to transfer that rich knowledge of a teacher Sentence
Transformer model to the student model so that we can obtain a student Sentence Trans-
former that can encode semantic information of a sentence better for the other languages.
By utilizing this component, we enhance the student model by helping it learning the
way semantic meaning is represented in the teacher model, which is usually more eﬀicient
due to being trained on a larger dataset. As a result, it help improve the performance of
the student model which often performs poorly due to lack of training resources. Also, as
state in [40], the distillation training only needs to focus on the translation knowledge as
a general textual encoder, so we can use bitext data to train the student query encoder,
which is more feasible for low resource languages. The execution flow of this component
is as follows:

Figure 4.2: Execution flow of the Knowledge Distillation component
1. First, we need parallel data of sentences in the source language, the language that
was used for training in the monolingual retrieval phase, and in the target language
in which we need to distill knowledge. For each pair of parallel sentences (s, t), s
will be fed into the teacher model, while t will be fed into the student model. The
teacher and student Sentence Transformers will then calculate token embeddings of
the tokens of the sentences passed into them. We denote E(.) and ˆE(.) the token
embeddings of the teacher and student models, respectively.
2. As stated in the purpose of this component, we need to ensure that tokens that are
translation of each other should have similar embeddings, i.e, for a token si and its
translation token tj, we should have E(si) ≈ˆE(tj). In other words, we first find a
mapping f that maps a token in s with its corresponding token that has the same
meaning in t, then we minimize the average cosine distance between each mapped
pair. In order to achieve this, we will use the same approach as in proposed [40]. We
view this problem as an Optimal Transport problem. We consider each sentence to
be a distribution of tokens, the mapping f to be the transportation plan, and the
distance that needs minimizing to be the transportation cost. To elaborate further,
we assign mass to the tokens by considering each token to contain a proportion of
the whole sentence’s meaning. This part is also where our approach differs from
that mentioned in [40], which consider the token distribution to be uniform. As
sentences are tokenized differently in different languages, one token in a language
may correspond to more than one tokens in another language.
Therefore, if we
consider tokens to have the same mass, we cannot find a transportation plan that
maps the tokens correctly. Thus, to capture the semantic importance of each token in
s and t, we define a source distribution, µs, on s and a uniform target distribution,

4.2.2
Query Expansion
As mentioned in the Related Works chapter, PRF [2] [19] is an eﬀicient query expansion
method that relies only on the query itself and the document corpus, does not require
user feedback, and can be applied to all languages regardless of language characteristics.
Several variations of PRF have been created to improve this technique, including using
expectation maximization technique to extract expansion terms from the initially retrieved
top k documents [35] [38], recognizing and assigning different weights to parts of the
document [34] [35], or using the help of a resource-rich language [4] [5] [35].
In our current proposed model and pipeline, we will not use the approach of using the
help of a resource-rich language. There are two main reasons for this decision:
The first reason is the difference in the importance of this component. The approaches
in the cited papers use PRF and query expansion as the main steps in the information
retrieval process, so they need to focus on this step. Meanwhile, our approach considers the
query expansion component as a supporting step, which helps to match more documents
in the lexical similarity step and avoids the possibility of missing relevant documents.
Therefore, we want to design this component to work more simply to focus on the next
steps.
The second reason is about the performance of the model and pipeline. As mentioned
in the first reason, we aim for this component to be a support component, so we want it to
run faster so that we can focus on the following steps. Not using additional support from
another language will help avoid the additional time needed to translate queries from the
original language to the resource-rich language, perform resource-rich query expansion,
and combine the results of both languages. We will also avoid being dependent on the
translation model, and also avoid the need for additional datasets in the resource-rich
language.
For the two reasons mentioned above, we decided to choose an approach that focused
on the first two variants. We finally design our query expansion component as a variation
of a technique mentioned in [35], which is UnifiedStructPRF. The image below shows the
architecture and execution flow of this component:

4.2.3
Lexical Matching
As mentioned in Section 4.2, the goal of this step is to calculate the lexical similarity
between the query and each document in the document corpus, in order to identify doc-

The QueryExpansion class is implemented using the Expectation-Maximization (EM) al-

The constructor takes the input text dataset, separating the title and content into

ranker object from the rank_bm254 library is then initialized using the entire document

creating the necessary probability framework for the EM algorithm.
When a new query requires expansion, the system executes a two-phase process:
First, in the retrieval phase, any data from previous queries is cleared, and the

Next, in the expansion phase, the system applies the EM algorithm (mention in Sub-
section 4.2.2) iteratively to both the title and content relevant terms set. The algorithm
converges when the log likelihood change falls below the THRESHOLD_FOR_EM_ALGO, at
which point expansion term probabilities are finalized.

from both title and content sources are combined, ranked by probability, and the top

5.2.4
Lexical Matching
The LexicalMatching class implements lexical-relevant based retrieval method using the
BM25Plus algorithm. This component provides the list of top lexical-relevant document
for our query.
The initialization step begins by processing the provided document dataset to extract
document components (titles and contents) and their corresponding identifiers from the
dataset. For each document, the system combines its tokenized title and content to form
a unified document representation, which then be used to initialize the BM25Plus ranker.
The class also keeps a parallel list of document IDs that preserves the mapping between
the internal BM25Plus document indices and their original identifiers.
When a query requires document retrieval, we provide the tokenized query to the
get_documents_ranking method. Then we use the BM25Plus object we initialized to
perform BM25Plus algorithm to compute similarity scores between the query and every
document in the collection we provided. These raw numerical scores represent the lexical
similarity between each document and the query. We then sorts these scores in descending
order to identify the most relevant documents. For each document in this ranked order,
we retrieves the corresponding document identifier from the parallel ID list and pairs it
with the document’s relevance score. The final output is a sorted list of (document_id,
score) pairs, representing the most relevant documents for the query according to the
lexical similarity.
5.2.5

This class is designed to divide long documents into smaller, manageable chunks while
preserving the coherence of the content.
The main functionality of the class lies in its method to separate chunks from doc-
uments. This method processes a single document, defined by its ID, and splits it into
chunks based on the token length constraint. First, the document is retrieved from the
dataset by ID, and we take the title and content in a preprocessed form (split into sen-
tences). The sentences are processed iteratively, with a cumulative count of tokens main-
tained to determine when a chunk exceeds the specified limit. When the limit is reached,
the accumulated sentences are combined into a chunk and a new chunk is started. This
ensures that each chunk remains coherent, as it consists of full sentences rather than
arbitrary slices of text.

has been separated by the Chunk Separator component previously. Document chunks are
processed in batches, where each batch is converted into embeddings using the document-
specific model. The embeddings are compared to the query vector using cosine similarity.
A ReLU activation ensures non-negative similarity values, which are further transformed
into probabilities. The similarity scores of all chunks in a document are aggregated into
a single score, representing the document’s overall semantic relevance to the query.
For the forward pass, the class incorporates the “forward” method to combine semantic
similarity with pre-computed lexical or topic similarity scores. These scores are passed
through a linear layer followed by a sigmoid activation function to compute the final
similarity score for each document.

6.2
Evaluation Metric
In this evaluation setting, each query in our dataset is associated with only one known
relevant (true positive) document. This constraint makes it unsuitable to apply standard
ranking metrics such as Precision@k, Recall, or Mean Average Precision (MAP) as in [40],
which require multiple relevant documents per query to produce meaningful scores. As a
result, we adopt Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) as the primary evaluation metric, since
it only requires the rank of the first correct result, making it appropriate for scenarios
where only a single relevant answer is available for each query. Moreover, Accuracy@1
(Acc@1) and Accuracy@5 (Acc@5) are also utilized to evaluate our model, to assess how
often the correct result appears at the top or within the top 5 retrieved documents.
The Reciprocal Rank (RR) calculates the reciprocal of the rank at which the first
relevant document was retrieved. When averaged across queries, the measure is called
the Mean Reciprocal Rank (MRR) [7].

7.1
Achieved Results
Throughout this project, we have been exposed to new mathematical knowledge. We
have grasped the definitions and properties of Topology, Measure theory and Optimal
Transport. We were also able to prove these properties and provide specific examples to
illustrate the concepts and properties we have learned. Furthermore, we have reviewed and

and semantic scoring to effectively retrieve relevant content. It is optimized for eﬀiciency
and can be deployed on accessible hardware such as T4 GPUs.
To promote transparency, we have published our project’s source code on Github1.
This work lays the foundation for building effective retrieval systems in low-resource
languages, providing an open-source baseline for further advancement in multilingual and
low-resource IR research.
7.2
Limitations
While the proposed system demonstrates promising results in low-resource language re-
trieval, several limitations remain that impact its scalability and generalizability.
First, calculating similarity scores between a query and all document chunks, presents
a practical bottleneck. This limitation becomes more pronounced as the corpus size grows,
restricting the model’s responsiveness in real-time applications or large-scale deployments.
